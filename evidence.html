Overview

This page shares selected evidence from my teaching practice and Moreland University clinical work. I chose artifacts that show how I plan, teach, assess, reflect, and adjust instruction for young multilingual learners.

The focus is not to show every assignment I completed. Instead, I want this page to show the parts of my teaching that matter most in the classroom: early literacy, language development, routines, assessment, family communication, and helping young children build confidence step by step.

Teaching Evidence Areas

Early Literacy and Phonics

Phonics CVC Blending Decodable Reading Early Years

A major part of my recent teaching has focused on helping kindergarten students build early reading confidence. In my classroom, this often includes letter-sound review, oral blending, shoulder-elbow-wrist tapping, CVC word practice, decodable sentences, movement-based games, and short one-to-one checks.

I want early reading to feel possible for students. For young multilingual learners, that means giving them clear routines, repeated practice, and enough encouragement to keep trying when blending feels difficult. The goal is not only for students to read a word once, but to understand the routine well enough that they can use it again with more independence.

What this shows: I can teach early literacy through clear routines, movement, repetition, and responsive support for young multilingual learners.

Assessment-Informed Instruction

Assessment Progress Monitoring Instructional Adjustment

I use assessment to understand what students are ready for next. In early years classrooms, that often means watching carefully, listening to how students respond, checking workbooks, doing quick one-to-one reading checks, and noticing patterns across the class.

In this assessment cycle, I tracked a class of 16 multilingual kindergarten learners across three collection points. The focus areas were CVC decoding, CVC word recognition, and collaborative problem solving during partner work. The data showed growth across all three areas, but it also helped me see where students still needed more support. That balance matters to me because assessment should not only confirm what went well; it should help me plan what students need next.

Assessment Focus Data Collection 1 Data Collection 2 Data Collection 3 Growth

CVC Word Recognition 67.50% 75.00% 76.25% +8.75 percentage points
Collaborative Problem Solving During Partner Work 2.19 / 4 2.56 / 4 2.63 / 4 +0.44 on a 4-point scale

By the final data collection, 12 out of 16 students reached 80% or higher in CVC decoding, 11 out of 16 reached 80% or higher in CVC word recognition, and 10 out of 16 scored 3 or higher in collaborative problem solving. At the same time, the data reminded me that some students still needed more guided practice, especially when moving from isolated CVC skills into more connected or independent reading tasks.

This helped me see that students were making real progress, but that my next lessons still needed to include more guided blending, decodable sentence practice, and opportunities for students to use the routine with increasing independence. It also reminded me that partner work needs to be taught and practiced intentionally, especially with young learners who are still developing language, confidence, and self-regulation.

In the short /o/ performance task, students identified beginning, middle, and ending sounds, blended short /o/ words, and attempted a short decodable sentence. The results helped me see that many students were stronger with isolated sound work than with connected sentence reading, so my next steps needed to include more practice with connected reading, not just individual words.

What this shows: I use assessment as a practical teaching tool, not just a grade. I look for patterns, identify what students need next, and adjust instruction from there.

Differentiation for Multilingual Learners

EAL/ESL Visual Supports Scaffolding Inclusive Practice

My students often come into lessons with different English levels, confidence levels, attention needs, and developmental readiness. I use visuals, modeling, simplified language, movement, wait time, partner support, repeated routines, and one-to-one check-ins to help students participate successfully.

In my classroom, scaffolding is a way to help every student find a path into the lesson. For example, if I give students a simple sentence frame like “I like ___” with picture choices on the interactive whiteboard, even quieter students can join in with more confidence. Those supports reduce the fear of making mistakes and give students a clearer way to participate.

I also believe students’ home language can be a helpful resource, not something that has to disappear from the room. Sometimes students need to brainstorm in Chinese, hear a quick translation, or connect a new English word to something they already understand. When students feel safe and respected, they are usually more willing to take risks with English.

What this shows: I support multilingual learners by making language visible, usable, and less intimidating, while still helping students grow toward more independent English use.

Academic Vocabulary and Language Development

Vocabulary Language Development Concept Checking

Young English learners need vocabulary to be clear, visible, and connected to real use. Words like “predict” or “compare” can be difficult if they are only explained once, so I pre-teach important words with visuals and then bring them back through stories, questions, classroom routines, and activities.

I also use concept-checking questions and simple student responses to see whether students really understand the word, not just whether they can repeat it. For kindergarten students, language growth happens through repeated exposure, meaningful practice, and many small chances to use words in context.

What this shows: I teach vocabulary as part of real classroom communication, not as isolated word lists.

Classroom Culture, SEL, and Routines

SEL Classroom Routines Self-Regulation

Young learners need a classroom that feels caring, predictable, and active. I use songs, movement, visual routines, calm-down strategies, breathing resets, and consistent expectations to help students stay engaged and regulate their emotions.

In my classroom, warmth and structure work together. I want students to feel safe, but I also want them to understand the routines well enough to participate with confidence. When students are upset, distracted, or unsure, I try to help them reset and return to the learning instead of treating the behavior as the whole story.

What this shows: I build classroom culture through relationships, routines, and calm support, not only through rules or behavior correction.

Planning and Inquiry-Based Learning

Planning PYP-Informed Interdisciplinary Learning

My planning often connects literacy, vocabulary, movement, stories, science, social-emotional learning, and hands-on activities. I try to make lessons developmentally appropriate while still giving students meaningful language and ideas to work with.

In my teaching, inquiry does not always mean a big project or a complicated activity. With young learners, it can begin with a question, a story, a picture, a science idea, or a problem students can talk about and explore. I try to connect those moments back to language, confidence, and participation.

What this shows: I can plan lessons that connect language, content, movement, and curiosity in ways that fit young learners.

Family Communication

Parent Communication Feedback Student Growth

I believe family communication should be clear, specific, and encouraging. Parents should know what their child is learning, what progress I am seeing, and what they can practice at home without feeling overwhelmed.

In my parent communication, I try to describe the child’s actual learning behavior. For example, instead of only saying that a student is “doing well,” I might explain that the student knows letter sounds but needs more practice blending them smoothly, or that sentence reading is still harder than word reading. This helps families understand the next step in a practical way.

What this shows: I communicate with families in a way that is warm, specific, and connected to real student growth.

Featured Artifact: CVC Performance Assessment Case Study

One of my strongest artifacts is a short /o/ CVC performance assessment from my kindergarten class. Students practiced short /o/ words through daily routines and then completed a one-to-one reader conference. The task focused on phoneme identification, blending and decoding, word accuracy, and sentence reading.

This artifact shows a full teaching cycle. I planned a short literacy sequence, taught the routine through daily practice, assessed students one-to-one, looked for class patterns, and used those patterns to plan next steps. Across the three data collections, the class average improved in all three assessment focus areas. CVC decoding increased from 61.25% to 73.75%, CVC word recognition increased from 67.50% to 76.25%, and collaborative problem solving improved from 2.19 to 2.63 on a 4-point scale. The results showed progress, but they also reminded me that connected reading, independent use of the routine, and purposeful partner work still needed more support.
